III. Analysis of Lawsuits Filed Against the Fairburn Police Department
The research has identified two specific lawsuits filed against the City of Fairburn Police Department within the last ten years that have garnered public or legal record. These cases provide insight into the types of legal challenges the department has faced.
A. Case-by-Case Breakdown of Identified Lawsuits:
 * Jessie Nell Smith v. City of Fairburn, Georgia, et al. (2017)
   This lawsuit, heard by the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals in 2017, involved allegations brought by Jessie Nell Smith against Detective Israel of the Fairburn Police Department, the Chief of Police (James A. McCarthy, Jr.), and the City of Fairburn. Smith's claim centered on a violation of 42 U.S.C. § 1983, asserting that she was arrested without probable cause and in retaliation for her criticism of the Chief of Police, thereby infringing upon her rights under the First and Fourth Amendments to the U.S. Constitution.
   The circumstances leading to the lawsuit began in July 2013 when police responded to noise complaints between Smith and her neighbor, Ronnie Jordan. Following a second call from Smith regarding a rock thrown near her window, Officer Kenneth Hammock reported that Smith stated she was "going to kill" Jordan. Smith denied making this exact statement but admitted to saying she would defend herself and her property. The following day, Chief McCarthy instructed Detective Israel to investigate. Detective Israel interviewed Smith, who denied making threats but made statements such as having a pistol and feeling like killing her neighbor. Detective Israel also spoke with Officer Crawford, who recalled Smith making a conditional threat to shoot Jordan if her car was damaged.
   Subsequently, Detective Israel obtained an arrest warrant for Smith for terroristic threats, stating in the affidavit that Smith threatened to kill Ronnie Jordan, a threat reportedly heard by Officer Hammock. Smith was arrested, but a grand jury later declined to indict her, and the charges were dropped. Following this, Smith filed her lawsuit. The District Court initially granted summary judgment to the defendants. On appeal, the Eleventh Circuit affirmed this decision, concluding that Detective Israel had at least arguable probable cause, if not actual probable cause, to believe Smith made a terroristic threat under Georgia law. The court also dismissed Smith's First Amendment retaliatory arrest claim against Chief McCarthy and her state law false arrest claim against the City of Fairburn, both due to the finding of probable cause. This case suggests a point of contention regarding the balance between an individual's right to criticize public officials and the authority of law enforcement to make arrests based on perceived threats.
 * Daniel Crawford v. City of Fairburn, Georgia (2007)
   This case, decided by the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals in 2007, concerned a claim of retaliation under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Daniel Crawford, who held the position of Major in the Fairburn Police Department, alleged that the City retaliated against him for conducting an internal investigation into a sexual harassment complaint filed by Officer Louise Tallman against Sergeant James Smith.
   The context of the case involved an earlier EEOC charge filed by Officer Tallman against the City alleging age and sex discrimination based on Sergeant Smith's alleged harassment. Crawford began investigating a subsequent internal complaint by Tallman, expanding his investigation to include allegations of insubordination and failure to support the Department. Before Crawford completed his investigation, the EEOC determined that it was likely Tallman had experienced sexual harassment related to her earlier charge. Crawford submitted a report on his findings, which the City Administrator and City Clerk concluded showed no evidence of unlawful discrimination or harassment.
   The City of Fairburn asserted that Crawford's termination was based on several non-discriminatory reasons, including inaccuracies in his investigation of Tallman's complaints, issues related to traffic patrols on Interstate 85, complaints about officer pay, problems with dispatchers, and concerns about low morale and favoritism. Crawford argued that these reasons were pretextual and that the real motivation behind his termination was retaliation for his investigation, which the City Administrator had reportedly said would "open up a can of worms" and "get the City sued". Both the District Court and the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals granted summary judgment in favor of the City, finding that Crawford did not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the City's stated reasons for his termination were a pretext for unlawful retaliation. This case highlights the complexities of employment law within a police department setting and the challenges in proving retaliation claims when an employer provides alternative justifications for adverse employment actions.

Understanding the provisions of the GTCA is essential for analyzing any tort claims filed against the Fairburn Police Department, as it dictates the conditions under which the City can be held liable.
