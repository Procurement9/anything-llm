# Analysis and Comparison of the Two Declarations of Independence

## Executive Summary

The original **Declaration of Independence (1776)** and the document titled **A 2nd Declaration of Independence (2021)** are both framed as foundational declarations rejecting tyrannical rule, yet they differ fundamentally in their historical context, intended audience, and philosophical assertions regarding governance. While the 1776 Declaration severed ties with the British Crown to establish a sovereign nation based on natural rights and consent of the governed, the 2nd Declaration asserts that the United States was secretly converted into a foreign-owned corporation (US CORP) in 1871 and seeks to reclaim sovereignty from a "Deep State" and globalist elite.

## Perspectives on Governance

### The 1776 Declaration of Independence
The original Declaration, drafted primarily by Thomas Jefferson and adopted by the Second Continental Congress, articulates a philosophy of governance rooted in Enlightenment ideals. It asserts that governments derive their "just powers from the consent of the governed" and exist primarily to secure unalienable rights, namely "Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness." When a government becomes destructive of these ends, it is the right and duty of the people to alter or abolish it. The document outlines a vision of a Constitutional Republic (though not explicitly named as such until the Constitution) where power is decentralized and representative.

### The 2nd Declaration of Independence
The 2nd Declaration, attributed to "WE CITIZENS OF AMERICA" and directed at the "SHAREHOLDERS OF US CORP," presents a conspiratorial view of American history. It claims that following the Civil War, a cabal of foreign investors financed America's recovery in exchange for administrative control, culminating in the creation of a corporate entity in 1871 that supplanted the Constitutional Republic. According to this document, the current government is not a representative body but a corporate management structure ("US CORP") serving foreign interests, including the City of London and the Vatican. It vehemently rejects Democracy, describing it as "mob rule" and a precursor to socialism and a "New World Order," advocating instead for a return to what it views as the original Constitutional Republic.

## Abuses of Oaths of Office and Power

### Grievances in 1776
The 1776 Declaration lists 27 specific grievances against King George III, characterizing his actions as a "history of repeated injuries and usurpations" aimed at establishing an "absolute Tyranny." Key abuses included:
- Refusing assent to necessary laws and obstructing the administration of justice.
- Dissolving representative houses and suspending legislatures.
- Imposing taxes without consent and depriving citizens of trial by jury.
- Maintaining standing armies in times of peace and rendering the military superior to civil power.

### Grievances in the 2nd Declaration
The 2nd Declaration lists 33 points outlining the alleged hijacking of the American government. It accuses elected officials and "OPERATIVES" of treason and abusing their oaths of office by serving corporate and foreign interests rather than the American people. Key abuses cited include:
- The establishment of the Federal Reserve in 1913, surrendering control of currency to foreign investors.
- The implementation of the 16th and 17th Amendments, which it claims were illegitimate and designed to tax labor and weaken state power.
- The orchestration of the 1933 bankruptcy by President Franklin D. Roosevelt, allegedly transferring America's assets to US CORP and forcing citizens to use fiat currency.
- The creation of the United Nations and the promotion of a "New World Order" to erode national sovereignty.
- The manipulation of elections, media, and education by the "Deep State" to brainwash citizens and destroy American culture.

## Conclusion

Both documents utilize the rhetorical structure of declaring independence from a tyrannical power. However, the 1776 Declaration addressed tangible political and economic grievances against a recognized monarch, establishing a new framework for democratic governance. In contrast, the 2nd Declaration relies on a sovereign citizen ideology, asserting that the legitimate government was secretly replaced by a corporation. It accuses modern officials of treason for upholding this corporate structure, demanding a bloodless return of assets and the dissolution of "US CORP" to restore the perceived original republic.
